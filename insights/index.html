About the Project

In June 2018, Luminate commissioned Simply Secure to conduct human- centered research focused on understanding grantees’ experiences of the funding process. This is the public report from that research - a collaboration between Luminate, formerly the Governance & Citizen Engagement (GCE) Initiative at Omidyar Network, and Simply Secure. The interviews and survey were conducted over the course of July - September 2018.

Learn more about the project

Simply Secure is a US 501(c)3 non-profit building a community of practitioners that puts people at the center of privacy, security, and transparency. We have core team members in New York City, Swarthmore, Berlin, and Toronto. Our small but nimble team collaborates with a global network of professionals to engage domain- specific talent for each project. Read more about Simply Secure and follow Simply Secure on Twitter.

Luminate does this by funding and supporting non-profit and for-profit organizations and advocating for policies and actions that will help people participate in and shape the issues affecting their lives, and make those in power more transparent, responsive, and accountable. Insights

From the interviews and surveys, nine key insights emerged around the grantee experience. Alongside each insight are anonymized quotes from the interviews, a description, related recommendations and any relevant resources related to the methodology and the insight or recommendations themselves.

  • all funding-approach-process trust-power
    grantees-value-deep-and-trusting-relationships-with-funders-grounded-in-subject-area-expertise

    Grantees value deep and trusting relationships with funders, grounded in subject area expertise.

  • all funding-approach-process funder-identity-culture
    when-funders-wear-multiple-hats-as-individual-advocates-as-well-as-representatives-of-a-funding-organization-it-can-be-unclear-which-role-the-funder-is-playing-at-any-given-time

    When funders wear multiple hats — as individual advocates as well as representatives of a funding organization — it can be unclear which role the funder is playing at any given time.

  • all trust-power funder-identity-culture
    a-funder-s-reputation-impacts-the-funder-grantee-relationship

    A funder’s reputation impacts the funder-grantee relationship.

  • all trust-power funder-identity-culture
    power-dynamics-if-not-acknowledged-can-affect-the-strength-and-quality-of-funder-grantee-relationships

    Power dynamics, if not acknowledged, can affect the strength and quality of funder-grantee relationships.

  • all trust-power funder-identity-culture
    a-lack-of-transparency-around-how-diversity-equity-and-inclusion-dei-is-valued-and-integrated-into-a-funder-s-work-can-lead-to-questions-about-a-funder-s-commitment-to-dei

    A lack of transparency around how diversity, equity and inclusion (DEI) is valued and integrated into a funder’s work can lead to questions about a funder’s commitment to DEI.

  • all funding-approach-process
    multi-year-flexible-funding-is-extremely-valued

    Multi-year flexible funding is extremely valued.

  • all funding-approach-process trust-power funder-identity-culture
    grantees-crave-structure-and-clarity-with-regards-to-1-the-funding-process-and-what-to-expect-from-the-relationship-once-funded-and-2-shifting-strategic-priorities

    Grantees crave structure and clarity with regards to 1) the funding process and what to expect from the relationship once funded, and 2) shifting strategic priorities.

    Grantees are interested in and looking for portfolio support, and would like more clarity, autonomy, and choice in how they access and receive that support.
